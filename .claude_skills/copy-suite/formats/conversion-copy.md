# Conversion Copy

## Workflow

1. Identify the format (sales page, email, ad, popup, CTA)
2. Name the one action you want the reader to take
3. Determine their awareness level (Schwartz's 5 levels)
4. Select the matching approach
5. Draft with the appropriate structure
6. Run the output quality gate (see SKILL.md Quality Gate section)

## Schwartz's 5 Awareness Levels (Core Decision Framework)

| Level | Reader State | Copy Approach |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Unaware | Doesn't know they have a problem | Lead with story or identification |
| Problem-aware | Knows the pain, not the solution | Agitate the problem, then introduce |
| Solution-aware | Knows solutions exist, not yours | Differentiate. Why you, not them? |
| Product-aware | Knows your product, hasn't bought | Handle objections, add proof, create urgency |
| Most-aware | Ready to buy, needs a push | Direct offer, deal, deadline |

The longer the copy, the less aware the reader. Most-aware readers need short, direct copy. Unaware readers need long-form education.

## Sales Page Architecture

### Long-Form (Low awareness, high price)
1. Headline (big promise or bold identification)
2. Lead (story, problem, or provocative claim)
3. Problem agitation (make the pain vivid)
4. Solution introduction (the bridge)
5. Product reveal (what it is, what it does)
6. Benefits stack (outcomes, not features)
7. Social proof (testimonials with specifics)
8. Objection handling (FAQ-style or woven into copy)
9. Value stack (everything they get, with prices)
10. Price anchor + reveal
11. Guarantee / risk reversal
12. Final CTA + urgency
13. PS (restate the key benefit or deadline)

### Short-Form (High awareness, low friction)
1. Headline (direct value prop)
2. 3-5 bullet benefits
3. Social proof snippet
4. CTA + guarantee

## Email Sequence Patterns

### Welcome Sequence (5-7 emails)
1. Deliver the promise (what they signed up for)
2. Origin story (build connection)
3. Quick win (teach something useful)
4. Social proof (who else uses this)
5. Soft pitch (here's how we can help further)

### Launch Sequence
1. Story/problem (seed the need)
2. Teaching (give value, build authority)
3. Announcement (the offer)
4. FAQ/objections (handle resistance)
5. Social proof (results from others)
6. Deadline (real urgency, real consequences)

### Cart Abandonment
- Email 1 (1hr): "Did something go wrong?" (helpful, not salesy)
- Email 2 (24hr): Restate the value + address top objection
- Email 3 (48-72hr): Urgency or incentive (discount, bonus)

## Ad Copy (Platform Constraints)

| Platform | Primary Text | Headline | Key Constraint |
|----------|-------------|----------|---------------|
| Meta/FB | 125 chars visible (before "see more") | 40 chars | Hook in first line or they scroll |
| Google Search | 30 char headlines x3, 90 char descriptions x2 | Must match intent | Keywords in headline, benefit in description |
| X/Twitter | 280 chars (or promoted long-form) | N/A | Native tone, don't sound like an ad |

**The 3-Second Rule:** On social, you have 3 seconds. The first line is either a hook or a corpse.

## Urgency Calibration

- **Real urgency** (deadline, limited stock, price increase): State it plainly. Real urgency doesn't need exclamation marks.
- **Manufactured urgency** (fake countdown, fake scarcity): Don't. It erodes trust and trains readers to ignore you.
- **Implied urgency** (opportunity cost, "every day you wait"): Effective when the cost of inaction is real.

## Price Presentation

- **Anchor high first.** Show what this would cost elsewhere or if built piece by piece.
- **Decoy pricing.** Three tiers where the middle tier looks obviously best.
- **Daily reframe.** "$97/year" becomes "less than a coffee every three days."
- **Value stack.** List everything included with individual prices, then show total vs. actual price.
- **Risk reversal.** Money-back guarantee should be confident, not apologetic. "If this doesn't [outcome], you get every penny back."
