# Long-Form Social Copy

## Workflow

1. Name the one job (establish authority? Tell a story? Change a belief? Drive action?)
2. Identify the platform (X long-form, LinkedIn, other)
3. Write the hook (first 2-3 lines visible before expand)
4. Map the emotional arc (where does the reader start emotionally? Where do they end?)
5. Draft the full post
6. Format for mobile readability
7. Run the output quality gate (see SKILL.md Quality Gate section)

## The Hook (First 2-3 Lines)

On every platform, only the first few lines are visible before "see more" or expand. This is the most critical real estate in the entire post.

**Hook patterns that earn the click:**
- Bold statement: "I lost everything at 28. It was the best thing that happened to me."
- Vulnerability: "I almost quit last week. Here's what changed my mind."
- Surprising data: "We analyzed our last 100 posts. 3 got 80% of our engagement."
- Story opening: "Last month, a stranger sent me a DM that changed how I think about X."
- Contrarian: "The advice everyone gives about X is making things worse."

**What kills the click:** Starting with context ("So I've been thinking about..."), starting with "I'm excited to announce," generic opening ("In today's fast-paced world").

## Emotional Architecture

Great long-form posts take the reader on an emotional journey:

```
Setup (identify, connect)
  ↓
Valley (tension, problem, vulnerability)
  ↓
Turn (insight, realization, breakthrough)
  ↓
Rise (new understanding, transformation)
  ↓
Landing (takeaway, call to action)
```

The valley is where the reader leans in. Don't rush past it. Sit in the tension. The turn should feel earned, not forced.

## Post Types

| Type | Structure | When to Use |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Personal narrative + lesson** | Story → what happened → what I learned | Building connection, showing growth |
| **Contrarian argument** | Common belief → why it's wrong → evidence → better way | Establishing authority, driving discussion |
| **Industry analysis** | Observation → data/patterns → what it means → what to do | Thought leadership |
| **How I did X** | Context → approach → result → what I'd do differently | Practical credibility |
| **Framework post** | Problem → mental model → how to apply it → examples | Bookmarks, high utility |

## Formatting for Readability

- **Short paragraphs.** 1-3 sentences max. On mobile, a 4-sentence paragraph looks like a wall.
- **Line breaks as breathing room.** Use them between ideas, not just between paragraphs.
- **Bold for emphasis.** Sparingly. If everything is bold, nothing is.
- **No bullet lists in the middle of a story.** Bullets break narrative flow. Use them for practical lists only.
- **One idea per paragraph.** If a paragraph makes two points, split it.

## Platform Nuances

### X/Twitter Long-Form
- First 280 characters are the preview. Optimize like a hook tweet.
- Long-form posts compete with threads. Use long-form when the content flows as one continuous piece and threads when each point stands alone.
- No character limit, but respect attention. 500-1500 words is the sweet spot.

### LinkedIn
- Professional vulnerability performs best. Career lessons, leadership insights, "what I learned managing X."
- Algorithm rewards dwell time. Longer, engaging posts that keep people reading get distributed more.
- Avoid: humblebrags, engagement pods, "agree?" endings, emoji walls.

## Robert Greene Readability Standard

Apply to every long-form piece:
- **Invisible structure.** The reader shouldn't feel the outline. Transitions are seamless, not mechanical.
- **Every paragraph earns its place.** If you can remove a paragraph and the piece still works, the paragraph shouldn't be there.
- **Authority through specificity.** Vague claims feel weak. Specific examples, numbers, and named situations feel authoritative.
- **Rhythm variation.** Short sentences. Then longer ones that carry the reader forward with momentum and detail. Then short again. Like breathing.
