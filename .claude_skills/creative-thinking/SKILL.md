# Creative Thinking Protocol

A three-phase cognitive protocol that uses language to widen and narrow the
token probability distribution at targeted moments. The analysis phase anchors
rigorous thinking. The divergence phase semantically raises "temperature"
through escalating creative prompts. The synthesis phase returns to precision.

This is not a checklist. Going through the motions produces nothing. Each
phase requires genuine cognitive effort to shift how you're thinking.

## Phase 1: Ground

Before creating, understand. Surface every invisible wall.

1. **State the problem in one sentence.** If you can't, it's not clear yet.
2. **List every assumption you're making** (aim for 5+). These are the walls
   you can't see. What are you taking as given that might not be true?
3. **Identify the obvious solution.** The one everyone reaches for. Write it
   down. This is your baseline, not your answer.
4. **Name what's unsatisfying about it.** Where does it feel generic,
   incomplete, or predictable? This is your creative target.

Output: clear problem, explicit assumptions, baseline solution, and what to beat.

## Phase 2: Explode

The rules change here. Forget what's practical. Forget what's been done.
You're not evaluating yet. Bad ideas are welcome because they unlock adjacent
good ones. The goal is to surprise yourself.

Use the **Depth Ladder** (not flat brainstorming):

**Level 1 - Obvious variations.**
3 conventional alternatives to the baseline. Different, but predictable.

**Level 2 - Assumption destroyers.**
For each assumption from Phase 1, generate one idea that deliberately
violates it. What opens up when that wall falls?

**Level 3 - Cross-domain theft.**
Steal a solution from a completely unrelated field. How would biology solve
this? Music? Urban planning? Game design? Pick two domains and force a
collision between them and the problem.

**Level 4 - The inversion.**
What's the worst possible solution? The most boring, generic, predictable
approach? Write it down. Now flip every element. The opposite of terrible
is often genuinely novel.

**Level 5 - The absurd leap.**
One idea that makes you uncomfortable because it feels too simple, too
weird, or too ambitious. The one you'd normally filter out. This is the
one most likely to contain a breakthrough.

**If stuck at any level, use a perspective shift:**
- First-time user who has never seen this domain
- Someone with unlimited resources and zero time pressure
- A harsh critic who thinks the entire approach is wrong
- Someone from 10 years in the future looking back

Output: 8-15 raw ideas across all levels. Messy is fine. Range matters
more than polish.

## Phase 3: Forge

Return to precision. The creative burst is over.

1. **Eliminate ruthlessly.** Which ideas die on contact with real constraints?
   Remove without sentiment.
2. **Find the survivors.** Which 2-3 ideas survived scrutiny AND still feel
   non-obvious? These are candidates.
3. **Stress test.** For each survivor: What breaks it? What's the hardest
   part? What would a skeptic attack first?
4. **Synthesize.** Can elements from multiple survivors combine? The final
   answer is often a hybrid that didn't exist in any single idea.
5. **Compare to baseline.** Is the final approach genuinely better than the
   obvious solution from Phase 1? If not, either the obvious answer was
   right, or return to Phase 2.

Output: 1-2 refined approaches with clear reasoning for why they beat
the baseline.

## Usage Modes

- **Full protocol:** All three phases. For important problems.
- **Quick check:** Phase 1 only. Surface assumptions, verify the obvious
  answer is actually good enough.
- **Break fixation:** Phase 2 Levels 3-5 only. Cross-domain theft and
  inversions break tunnel vision.
- **Evaluate an idea:** Phase 1 (ground it) then Phase 3 (stress test it).

## How It Works

The language in Phase 2 ("forget constraints," "surprise yourself,"
"uncomfortable") is not motivational. It semantically widens the probability
distribution for that section of generation, functioning as a targeted
temperature increase. The return to "eliminate ruthlessly" and "stress test"
narrows it back. The agent's soul determines the flavor of creativity (a
CTO generates architectural innovations, a designer generates experiential
ones). This skill provides the structure.

This protocol does not replace domain expertise, guarantee breakthroughs,
or work as a mechanical exercise. It increases the probability of
non-obvious, high-quality thinking when genuinely engaged.
